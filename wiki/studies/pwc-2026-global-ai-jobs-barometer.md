PwC's comprehensive analysis of over one billion job advertisements examining AI's impact on the global labor market.

## Update: 2026-06-27

This update incorporates detailed analysis of all 16 figures from the report. Key empirical findings include: A two-track jobs market has emerged where 52% of jobs are being democratized (AI automates more expert tasks, leaving less expert tasks for humans) and 22% are being professionalized (AI automates less expert tasks, leaving more expert tasks for humans) (Figure 2). Entry-level job postings in the highest AI exposure quartile have flatlined since 2022 (coinciding with ChatGPT's release), while lower exposure quartiles continue robust growth (Figure 7). Skills in AI-exposed roles are changing 2.2 times faster than in least exposed roles, with the gap widening annually (Figure 9). New tasks added to AI-exposed roles since 2022 are 2.5 times more likely to rely on human-intensive capabilities like empathy and creativity, quantified through the [[epoch-score]] (Figure 10). The report identifies a pronounced 'superstar effect' where the top 20% of AI-exposed companies achieve 5 times higher productivity growth (163% average) than the most exposed companies as a whole (33.5% in 2025) (Figure 12). Wage premiums for AI skills average 62% across sectors, ranging from 118% in Consumer Markets to 16% in Government (Figure 15). AI specialist job postings are growing eight times faster than total job postings, with Technology, Media and Telecoms leading at 11.4% of sector roles (Figure 16). These findings, derived from PwC's [[pwc-ai-occupational-exposure-index]], demonstrate AI's asymmetric impact on labor markets and validate the [[ai-job-future-framework]]'s predictive power.
