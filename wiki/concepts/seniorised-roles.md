# Seniorised Roles

Seniorised roles refer to entry-level positions that have evolved to require skills and capabilities traditionally associated with more senior positions, due to the impact of AI adoption. This phenomenon represents a fundamental shift in early career expectations and pathways.

## Definition and Identification

A role is classified as "seniorised" when an entry-level position now requires traditionally senior skills that were not previously expected at that career stage. According to PwC's 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer:

- A skill is defined as "traditionally senior" if it had more than 50 mentions in experienced (non-entry-level), high AI exposure job postings in 2019 but five or fewer mentions in entry-level, high AI exposure postings from the same year
- An entry-level job posting is classified as "seniorised" if it contains ten or more mentions of skills that are both new and traditionally senior

## Key Characteristics

Seniorised roles typically require capabilities such as:

- Motivational leadership
- Process management
- Team building
- Mentorship
- People management
- Stakeholder management
- Data-driven decision making

These capabilities demand emotional intelligence, judgment, and leadership ability at a level not historically required in many junior roles.

## Statistical Evidence

PwC's analysis reveals significant patterns related to seniorised roles:

- The most AI-exposed entry-level jobs are seven times more likely to require traditionally senior skills than the least AI-exposed ones
- For highly AI-exposed entry-level jobs, senior capabilities now account for 52% of new skills required (vs. 7% for least exposed)
- Seniorised entry-level roles are growing at 35% between 2019-2025, while non-seniorised roles are declining by 10%

## Impact on the Labor Market

The seniorisation phenomenon has created a divergence in entry-level job trajectories:

- Entry-level jobs in the highest AI exposure quartile have flatlined since 2022 (coinciding with ChatGPT's release)
- Only seniorised entry-level roles within high AI exposure fields are experiencing growth
- Early-career workers must now develop advanced capabilities much earlier in their careers

## Strategic Implications

For organizations, seniorised roles necessitate:
- Redesigned onboarding and training programs
- Accelerated mentorship and development pathways
- Revised expectations for early-career performance

For workers, particularly those early in their careers:
- Opportunity to skip years of routine tasks
- Challenge of needing to demonstrate leadership and strategic thinking sooner
- Increased importance of developing human-intensive skills alongside technical capabilities
