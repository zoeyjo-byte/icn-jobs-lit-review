## Update: 2026-06-27

The 'superstar effect' identifies a critical divergence among AI adopters: the top 20% of most AI-exposed firms achieve dramatically higher productivity gains than peers. This effect was documented by [[pwc]] using [[orbis]] company financial data. As revealed in Figure 12 of the PwC 2026 report, these 'superstar' companies attained 163% average productivity growth in 2025—5 times higher than the 33.5% growth rate of most AI-exposed companies as a whole. This advantage has widened steadily since 2022, with superstars accelerating their lead while other AI-exposed firms maintain moderate growth. Figure 13 shows this productivity advantage translates to headcount growth, with superstar firms expanding employment 52.2% by 2025 versus 35.7% for least exposed quartiles. Crucially, Figure 14 demonstrates these gains are shared with workers through higher wage growth (24.4% vs 16.6% in 2025). The report attributes superstar status to three factors: strategic AI integration beyond automation (leveraging the [[ai-job-future-framework]]), investment in complementary human skills, and organizational agility in addressing [[ai's limitations]]. This creates a 'winner-takes-most' dynamic where superstars capture disproportionate value—evident in Figure 16's AI specialist hiring surge within these firms. The effect has profound implications: companies failing to achieve superstar status risk falling behind in the AI transformation, while workers in superstar firms gain through both wages and career opportunities in high-growth [[professionalised-jobs]]. Policymakers must address resulting market concentration through targeted support for mid-tier AI adopters.
