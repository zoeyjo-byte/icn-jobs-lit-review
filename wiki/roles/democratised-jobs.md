## 2026-06-25 Update

Democratised jobs (52% of global positions) are defined by AI automating expert tasks, leaving less expert work for humans. PwC's analysis shows these roles grow at 17% since 2018 but face critical constraints: skill demand growth lags at 33% (vs 68% for professionalised), and salary growth is 42% slower (26% vs 37%). The data reveals a paradox - while volume expands, individual roles face deskilling pressures, with 61% of new positions requiring fewer specialized credentials than pre-AI equivalents.

In market research, democratised roles include 'AI Survey Technicians' who manage automated data collection systems (replacing manual programming) and 'Basic Insight Reporters' who compile AI-generated findings. These positions show strong volume growth (24% annually) but declining wages in real terms outside superstar firms. The EPOCH score (0.48) indicates high human intensity, but primarily in routine interpersonal tasks like participant scheduling rather than strategic judgment.

Sector analysis shows democratisation is most advanced in consumer-facing fields: 73% of new retail research roles involve AI-managed customer feedback systems. However, the report warns of saturation risks - entry-level democratised positions in the highest AI exposure quartile have flatlined since 2022. Crucially, these roles increasingly serve as on-ramps to professionalised tracks; 48% of democratised hires transition within 18 months by developing skills in [[ai-interpretation-frameworks]] and ethical oversight. Geographic trends show fastest democratisation in emerging economies (India +31%, Brazil +29%), though wage premiums remain concentrated in advanced economies. For career resilience, workers should combine democratised role efficiency with deliberate upskilling toward professionalised capabilities like [[human-intensive-skills]].
