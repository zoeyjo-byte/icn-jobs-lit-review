## Update: 2026-06-27

Democratised jobs—where AI automates more expert tasks, leaving less expert tasks for humans—constitute 52% of global postings but show distinct market patterns. Figure 2 illustrates this through roles like [[inventory-clerks]] (AI automates inventory management but humans move stock) and [[recruiters]] (AI screens CVs but humans negotiate contracts). While numerous, Figure 4 shows they've added skills at half the pace of professionalised roles (33% vs 68% growth 2018-2025), and Figure 5 confirms slower salary growth (26% vs 37% since 2021). However, Figure 11 reveals a critical nuance: democratised roles maintain the highest average [[epoch-score]] (0.48), indicating continued reliance on human capabilities for remaining tasks—often involving physical dexterity, adaptability in unstructured environments, or interpersonal interactions. This explains why these roles remain resilient despite reduced expertise requirements. The report warns that while democratisation expands job accessibility (e.g., enabling non-experts to perform complex analysis via AI tools), it may compress career ladders without structured progression paths. Figure 9 contextualizes this within skills transformation, showing democratised roles still experience 2.2x faster skill changes than low-exposure jobs. Organizations can leverage this trend through the [[ai-job-future-framework]]'s 'Environmental forces' dimension, designing roles that combine AI efficiency with uniquely human execution—particularly valuable in sectors like retail and logistics where physical interaction remains essential.
