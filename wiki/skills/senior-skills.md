# Senior Skills in Entry-Level Roles

Senior skills refer to capabilities traditionally required in more senior positions that are now increasingly appearing in entry-level job descriptions due to AI adoption. This phenomenon, documented in PwC's 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer, represents a significant shift in early career expectations.

## Definition and Identification

A skill is defined as "traditionally senior" if it had more than 50 mentions in experienced (non-entry-level), high AI exposure job postings in 2019 but five or fewer mentions in entry-level, high AI exposure postings from the same year.

An entry-level job posting is classified as "seniorised" if it contains ten or more mentions of skills that are both new (having more than 10 mentions in 2025 but five or fewer in 2019) and traditionally senior.

## Key Senior Skills Now Required in Entry-Level Positions

The most common senior skills now appearing in AI-exposed entry-level roles include:

- **Motivational leadership**: Inspiring and guiding others toward shared goals
- **Process management**: Designing, implementing, and optimizing workflows
- **Team building**: Creating cohesive, high-performing groups
- **Mentorship**: Guiding and developing less experienced colleagues
- **People management**: Overseeing staff performance, development, and engagement
- **Stakeholder management**: Building and maintaining relationships with key parties
- **Data-driven decision making**: Using analytics to inform strategic choices

## Statistical Evidence

PwC's analysis reveals that the most AI-exposed entry-level jobs are now seven times more likely to require traditionally senior skills than the least AI-exposed ones. For entry-level jobs highly exposed to AI, these senior capabilities now account for 52% of new skills required, compared to just 7% for entry-level jobs least exposed to AI.

This shift has created a divergence in job growth patterns:
- Seniorised entry-level roles: 35% growth between 2019-2025
- Non-seniorised entry-level roles: -10% growth during the same period

## Strategic Implications

For early-career workers, this represents both opportunity and challenge. The positive aspect is being spared years of routine tasks, but the challenge is needing to develop advanced capabilities much earlier in their careers.

Organizations must reinvent early career pathways by redesigning onboarding, mentorship, and training programs to accelerate development of these advanced skills. Educators should also adapt curricula to prepare students for these evolving expectations.
