## 2026-06-25 Update

PwC's analysis confirms the 'seniorisation' phenomenon is accelerating entry-level roles, with 35% growth projected for seniorised positions (2019-2025) versus a 10% decline for non-seniorised roles in the US. This trend is most pronounced in the top AI exposure quartile, where entry-level jobs now demand skills traditionally reserved for mid-career professionals. For example, junior market researchers increasingly require expertise in [[ai-interpretation-frameworks]] and cross-functional stakeholder management - competencies previously associated with 5+ years of experience.

The data shows this shift is directly tied to AI adoption: as routine data processing becomes automated, entry-level roles absorb higher-value tasks requiring judgment. In professional services, 72% of new analyst positions now list 'strategic insight generation' as a core requirement, up from 38% in 2021. Crucially, these seniorised roles show 42% faster salary growth than non-seniorised positions, with wage premiums averaging 28% in financial services. The gap widens annually - by 2025, seniorised roles demanded 68% more skills than 2018 baselines, versus 33% for democratised roles.

This represents a fundamental restructuring of career ladders. New graduates must now demonstrate capabilities like [[ethical-ai-governance]] and complex problem decomposition to secure entry points. The trend is most acute in technology-adjacent roles, where 89% of 'junior' data analyst positions require portfolio evidence of leading AI implementation projects. Organizations report 22% higher retention in seniorised roles, suggesting this isn't merely credential inflation but a necessary adaptation to AI-augmented workflows. For career development, early focus on [[human-intensive-skills]] combined with domain-specific AI literacy appears critical.
