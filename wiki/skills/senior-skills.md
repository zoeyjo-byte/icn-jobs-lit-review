## Update: 2026-06-27

New data from the PwC 2026 report quantifies the acceleration of 'seniorised' entry-level roles. Figure 8 reveals that in the US top AI exposure quartile, entry-level positions requiring traditionally senior skills (strategic decision-making, complex problem-solving, leadership) are projected to grow 35% between 2019-2025, while non-seniorised roles decline by 10%. This phenomenon occurs because AI tools automate foundational tasks previously performed by junior staff—such as data entry for [[inventory-clerks]] or CV screening for [[recruiters]] (Figure 2)—effectively compressing career ladders. Consequently, new graduates must now demonstrate competencies typically associated with 3-5 years of experience. Key senior skills appearing in entry-level descriptions include advanced data interpretation, cross-functional collaboration, and client relationship management, particularly in high-growth [[professionalised-jobs]] like environmental engineering. Figure 7 contextualizes this within broader market trends, showing high AI exposure quartile entry-level postings have flatlined since 2022, suggesting seniorisation may be a prerequisite for growth in AI-impacted roles. The report warns this trend could exacerbate early-career inequality without structured mentorship programs, while creating opportunities for accelerated career progression in organizations embracing the [[ai-job-future-framework]].
