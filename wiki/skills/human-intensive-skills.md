Human-intensive skills refer to capabilities that are difficult to automate and increasingly valuable in AI-exposed roles, including empathy, creativity, critical thinking, complex communication, and face-to-face presence. These skills enable workers to handle atypical cases, provide emotional intelligence, and exercise judgment in ambiguous situations—areas where AI currently has limitations. As AI automates routine cognitive tasks, human-intensive skills become differentiators in the labor market, commanding growing wage premiums. They are particularly crucial in client-facing roles, innovation-driven positions, and complex decision-making contexts.

## Update: 2026-06-25
The PwC 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer (Figure 10) quantifies the premium on human-intensive skills. New tasks added to AI-exposed roles since 2022 are 2.5x more likely to rely on these capabilities, measured by the [[epoch-score]]. The top AI exposure quartile shows an average EPOCH score of 0.47 for new tasks versus 0.19 for the bottom quartile, indicating significantly greater human capability reliance.

Figure 11 reinforces this trend, showing democratised roles have the highest average EPOCH score (0.48) in 2025, followed by professionalised roles (0.45), while low-exposure roles score significantly lower (0.26). This pattern holds across both democratization and professionalization pathways, confirming that AI-augmented work—regardless of direction—increasingly centers on intrinsically human capabilities. The data suggests these skills are becoming foundational for AI-resilient careers, with implications for education systems and corporate training programs that must prioritize developing irreplaceable human strengths alongside technical competencies.
