## Update: 2026-06-27

The PwC 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer provides robust quantitative evidence for the accelerating criticality of human-intensive skills in AI-reshaped occupations. Figure 10 demonstrates that new tasks added to AI-exposed roles since 2022 are 2.5 times more likely to rely on capabilities like empathy, creativity, and face-to-face presence. Quantitatively, the top AI exposure quartile shows an average [[epoch-score]] of 0.47 for new tasks compared to 0.19 in the bottom quartile. This trend is reinforced in Figure 11, which reveals democratised roles (0.48) and professionalised roles (0.45) maintain significantly higher EPOCH scores than low exposure roles (0.26), indicating structural reliance on human capabilities despite AI automation. The report attributes this to AI handling standardized cognitive tasks while elevating the value of uniquely human interactions—particularly in client-facing roles like [[pharmacists]] and [[recruiters]] as shown in Figure 2. These skills now represent critical differentiators in high-exposure occupations, with implications for workforce development. Organizations increasingly seek candidates who can exercise emotional intelligence in complex scenarios where AI outputs require human validation, creating new career pathways in [[professionalised-jobs]]. The data suggests human-intensive capabilities will become non-negotiable requirements for roles experiencing significant AI transformation.
