## 2026-06-25 Update

New analysis from PwC's 2026 Global AI Jobs Barometer reveals that human-intensive skills are becoming increasingly critical in AI-reshaped roles. Tasks added to AI-exposed occupations since 2022 are 2.5 times more likely to require capabilities like empathy, creativity, and face-to-face presence compared to pre-AI tasks. The EPOCH score (measuring human capability reliance) for new tasks in the top 25% of AI-exposed roles averages 0.47, versus just 0.19 for the least exposed quartile. This divergence has accelerated since ChatGPT's release, with the skill transformation gap widening to 116% by 2025.

Democratised jobs (52% of all positions) show the highest EPOCH score at 0.48, indicating that as AI automates expert tasks like CV screening in recruitment, humans increasingly handle relationship-focused work such as client negotiation and complex candidate assessment. Similarly, professionalised roles like environmental engineering (EPOCH 0.45) now emphasize human judgment in atypical cases where AI outputs require validation. The data shows these skills are not merely complementary but essential differentiators - positions demanding high human intensity see 33% faster job growth in professionalised sectors.

Cross-referencing with [[wage-premiums-for-ai-skills]], we observe that while technical AI skills command high premiums, human-intensive capabilities create resilience against displacement. In healthcare roles, for instance, medical secretaries (democratised jobs) now focus almost exclusively on patient interaction after AI automated scheduling, requiring advanced emotional intelligence. Organizations should prioritize training in adaptive communication and ethical decision-making, as these skills show the strongest correlation with productivity growth in [[superstar-companies]]. The trend suggests that by 2027, human-intensive capabilities will define career advancement in 68% of market research roles.
